Can you explain the STCG from a redemption of Liquid fund? In layman language with examples?

In India, when you sell (redeem) units of a Liquid Fund, any profit you make is considered a Capital Gain. Since Liquid Funds are primarily used for short-term parking of cash, most investors deal with Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG).

Here is a breakdown of how it works in simple terms.

1. What counts as "Short-Term"?

For Liquid Funds (and all debt-oriented mutual funds), the definition of "short-term" changed significantly due to recent tax law updates:

  • For investments made ON or AFTER April 1, 2023: There is no "Long-Term" anymore. All gains, regardless of how long you hold the units (1 day or 5 years), are treated as Short-Term Capital Gains.

  • For investments made BEFORE April 1, 2023: Gains are short-term if you hold the units for 36 months (3 years) or less.

2. How is it Taxed?

The taxation is straightforward but can be heavy depending on your income:

  • The "Income Pile" Rule: STCG from Liquid Funds is added directly to your total annual income.

  • Tax Rate: You pay tax according to your applicable Income Tax Slab rate.

    • If you are in the 10% bracket, you pay 10%.

    • If you are in the 30% bracket, you pay 30%.

3. Real-World Examples

Example A: The High-Earner (30% Slab)

Imagine you are a professional earning a high salary, placing you in the 30% tax bracket.

  1. Investment: You put ₹1,00,000 into a Liquid Fund in May 2023.

  2. Redemption: You withdraw the money 6 months later for ₹1,04,000.

  3. Profit (STCG): ₹4,000.

  4. Tax Calculation: Since you are in the 30% slab, you owe 30% of that profit.

  5. Tax Paid: ₹1,200 (plus applicable cess).

Example B: The Student or Low-Income Earner (0% Slab)

Imagine you are a student or someone whose total annual income (including these gains) is below the basic exemption limit (e.g., ₹2.5L or ₹3L depending on the regime).

  1. Investment: You invest ₹50,000.

  2. Redemption: You withdraw it for ₹52,000.

  3. Profit (STCG): ₹2,000.

  4. Tax Calculation: Because your total income is below the taxable limit, you effectively pay ₹0 in tax on this gain.

Key Points to Remember

  • No Indexation: You cannot adjust your purchase price for inflation to reduce your tax. You pay on the "raw" profit.

  • TDS: Generally, Mutual Funds do not deduct tax (TDS) when an Indian Resident redeems units. You are responsible for declaring this income and paying the tax when you file your ITR (Income Tax Return).

  • Net Amount: Your bank account receives the full ₹1,04,000 (from Example A), but you must remember to set aside the tax amount for later.
